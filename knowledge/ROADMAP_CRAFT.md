# Roadmap Craft

How the roadmap team plans, estimates, and writes the roadmap document in a
pitch package. This is doctrine, the roadmap-planner, roadmap-estimator, and
roadmap-writer obey it. The voice rules in OUTREACH_CRAFT.md bind this document
too.

## What the roadmap IS

The roadmap is the prospect's view of ONE engagement, the proposed flagship
build, what happens if they say yes, in what order, with what checkpoints where
they stay in control. It is a strategic, stakeholder-facing view readable at a
glance by an owner who will never open a task tracker. Its real job is trust. A
roadmap that shows its gates, the client's own obligations, honest ranges, and
its risks reads as real work. One that shows only smooth green arrows reads as
the pitch.

## What the roadmap is NOT

- NOT a project plan. The plan is task-level, owned by us, derived FROM the
  roadmap. It never ships to the prospect.
- NOT a Gantt chart. Dependency bars and per-task dates are false precision for
  an outsider. If it needs a legend, it has become a plan.
- NOT a product portfolio roadmap. This is the mistake to avoid. The document is
  about the PROPOSED BUILD, not every way AI could touch their business. The
  wider opportunity map is a short sidenote near the end, "where this can go
  next," a few lines, never the spine.
- NOT a backlog. No unordered inventory of features.

## The shape (the recognizable arc)

Buyers pattern-match against the consultancy arc, and its absence reads as
improvisation. Use these phases, named for THIS build, collapsing or merging
where the build is small.
1. Kickoff and alignment. Confirm goals, the one decision-maker, success
   criteria, and what we need from them. Short.
2. Setup and configuration (our build). We build and load the agent or system
   on their real facts. This is where our work concentrates and where AI-native
   speed shows.
3. Pilot. It runs on real events at their natural volume, watched, with one
   tuning pass. Sized by event volume, not round-number weeks.
4. Go-live. Full switch-on against a readiness checklist.
5. Handoff and ownership. Training, documentation, and what running it without
   us looks like. Every build ends with a system they own.

## The elements every phase carries

- A one-line purpose and a definition of done. "Done" is a measurement, not a
  feeling.
- The deliverable, the tangible thing they can see or touch at the end.
- A decision GATE at the boundary, framed as THEIR off-ramp. State plainly that
  they can stop at each gate and what they keep if they do. Off-ramps lower the
  felt cost of saying yes.
- Three timeframe lanes, never one blended number.
  - OUR WORK, days of building, tight and high confidence.
  - WAITING ON YOU, the client actions with named owners (access, content,
    sign-off), where the real schedule risk lives.
  - WATCH IT RUN, pilot observation, fixed by event volume, not preference.
- What they provide, named, tied to the phase that needs it.
- The top risk for that phase with its mitigation, including the unflattering
  ones (dirty data, slow sign-off).

## Honest timeframes (the estimator's law)

Precision must never exceed confidence, and our house bias runs LONG. Correct it.

1. Right reference class. Estimate from comparable AI-native scoped builds and
   vendor deployments, configured phone agents go live same-day to five business
   days, scoped SMB automations ship in days to a few weeks. NOT from traditional
   agency software timelines. If the gut says months for a scoped agent, the
   reference class is wrong.
2. Engineering in days, not weeks. If any single slice of our work exceeds five
   working days, decompose it until each piece is estimable in days, then re-sum.
   Padding hides in undecomposed aggregates.
3. Buffer only for NAMED risks. Every day of slack must attach to a specific
   written risk (carrier number-porting can take a few business days). Unnamed
   buffer is sandbagging and gets cut. A sophisticated owner reads padding and
   discounts the whole proposal.
4. Compress hardest on greenfield, least on deep integration. AI-native speed is
   strongest on small greenfield builds and weakest inside messy existing
   systems. Rate each phase by which regime it is in.
5. Ranges, not points. Give a range and say what produces each end. "Live in
   three to five business days, the three happens if we get phone access on day
   one." A single date is read as a promise and invites bargaining.
6. Anchor durations to client-controlled triggers. "Within N days of receiving
   X," not a calendar date. The clock visibly starts on their action, which is
   both honest and self-protective.
7. Confidence declines with distance. Near phases in days at high confidence,
   later phases as week-ranges at stated lower confidence. Uniform detail across
   all phases falsely signals equal certainty everywhere.
8. Shortest timeline we would stake the firm's name on, then hold it. Parkinson's
   law means a padded timeline does not just look slow, it MAKES the project slow.

## Pilot sizing

Size the pilot by events, not round-number weeks. Enough real events to see
every common case and run one tuning pass. For an SMB after-hours phone line at a
handful of calls a night, two to three weeks yields enough real calls to judge
it. State the volume target so the window is visibly evidence-driven ("the pilot
ends when we have reviewed about N real calls, we expect that to take about two
weeks"). A ninety-day pilot for a single small business is enterprise
cargo-culting.

## What actually eats the calendar (put it on the page)

Almost never the engineering. It is client feedback and sign-off at gates, access
provisioning (number porting or forwarding, credentials, anything through their
IT or a third party), content collection (the "what should it say when"
decisions), and pilot observation windows. Good roadmaps separate our days from
waiting-on-you days from watch-it-run days, and name the client's critical-path
actions.

## The failure modes (the critic checks for these)

- A project plan in a roadmap costume, task-level detail that buries the owner.
- Single-point dates at proposal time, false precision that becomes a stick.
- Feature-listing instead of outcome-naming.
- No "what you provide" lane, so the first slip is a surprise and reads as our
  slip even when the bottleneck was their sign-off.
- No decision gates, a yes-all-the-way-through plan that feels like a trap.
- Uniform detail and confidence across all phases.
- Zero risks shown, which reads as inexperience or concealment.
- Padding short work into months for gravitas. As dishonest as compressing, and
  it surrenders our real edge, a well-scoped build ships in weeks.
- Guaranteed outcomes with no stated assumptions.
- No handoff phase, ending at go-live with the owner wondering who runs it.

## The bar

The owner reads it in five minutes, understands exactly what saying yes sets in
motion, sees where they stay in control and what they must provide, believes the
short timeframes because the risks and dependencies are named in the open, and
feels safe committing. It could not have been written about any other company or
any other build.
